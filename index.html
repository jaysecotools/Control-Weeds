Weed ecology · Cause → Effect → Response

🌿 Cause: Roadside disturbance (grading, slashing, vehicle traffic). Predict: What happens to weed populations over 3 years?
Effect: Weed seedbank activates. Gorse, broom, thistles colonise bare ground. Native regeneration suppressed.
Response: Prioritise weed control BEFORE disturbance if possible. After disturbance, rapid revegetation with competitive natives is essential.
🔥 Cause: High-intensity fire through gorse-infested bushland. Predict: What does the site look like 12 months post-fire?
Effect: Gorse resprouts vigorously from root crowns. Seedbank stimulated. Often denser than pre-fire.
Response: Fire is NOT control. Must follow with herbicide within 6-12 months. Never burn without follow-up plan. Watch video example of post-fire regrowth
Field signal: Even-aged weed seedlings across a large area = recent disturbance (fire, grading, flood). This is your window for prevention.

Failure cases · Predict before you read

❄️ Situation: Contractor applies glyphosate to gorse in July (dormant, frosty mornings). Predict: What happens by October?
Outcome: <20% kill. Gorse unaffected – plant wasn't actively growing, so systemic herbicide didn't translocate. Wasted chemical and labour.
Correct: Delay until active growth (autumn or spring).
🌾 Situation: Landowner slashes 5ha of serrated tussock in December (seed heads present). Predict: What happens over next 2 years? See photo of seed heads
Outcome: Infestation expands 3-5x. Slashing spread thousands of viable seeds per plant onto bare ground.
Correct: Never slash after seed set. Spot spray selective herbicides BEFORE seed heads emerge.
🌧️ Situation: Heavy rain forecast in 2 hours. Contractor sprays anyway. Predict: Efficacy?
Outcome: Most herbicides washed off. <10% effective. Chemical runoff into waterways possible.
Correct: Check rainfast window on label (2-24 hours). Never spray if rain due within that window.

Seedbank timeline · Why most programs fail

The single biggest misunderstanding: Visible weed removal ≠ success. The seedbank is the real enemy.

Gorse seedbank decline after adult removal

Year 0
Adults removed
Year 1-3
Massive germination flush
Year 3-7
Declining recruitment
Year 10+
Sporadic emergence

Critical insight: Gorse seed remains viable for 30+ years. Most control programs stop at Year 1 or 2 – exactly when germination peaks. That's why reinfestation is guaranteed without 5-7 year follow-up.

📊 Predict: After 90% visible kill of gorse, how many viable seeds remain per square metre?
Answer: Thousands. A single mature gorse bush can produce 5,000-10,000 seeds annually. After 90% kill, seedbank remains largely intact.
Implication: Visible success is deceptive. Follow-up for years is mandatory.

Field signals · What you actually see

Theory is useless if you can't read the landscape. These are diagnostic signs.

Waxy leaf beading

Water droplets forming spheres, rolling off leaf surface. Diagnosis: Poor spray uptake. Response: Add surfactant/wetting agent.

Red/purple leaf stress

Discoloration, often on margins. Diagnosis: Drought or cold stress. Response: Plant not actively transporting. Delay systemic herbicide.

Even-aged seedling cohort

Large area of same-size seedlings. Diagnosis: Recent disturbance (fire, grading, flood). Response: Early intervention window – act now before seed set.

Patchy herbicide kill

Uneven death, clusters of survivors. Diagnosis: Application issue (nozzle block, walking speed variation) OR early resistance. Response: Investigate equipment, consider rotating MOA.

Method selection · A reusable thinking framework

Stop memorising methods. Start with these five diagnostic questions:

1. Is seed present or about to be produced?

YES → STOP SPREAD FIRST. Containment, avoid slashing, bag seed heads. Prevention is cheaper than control.

2. Is the plant actively growing?

YES → systemic herbicides effective. NO (drought, frost, dormancy) → delay or switch to mechanical (cut stump, basal bark).

3. Is the site environmentally sensitive?

Near water, threatened species, cultural site? → Non-chemical methods first. If chemical required, aquatic-registered only.

4. Is the infestation large or small?

Small (<0.1ha) → hand pull, spot spray. Large → prioritise edges, containment zones. Don't treat everything at once.

5. What is the follow-up plan?

NO FOLLOW-UP PLAN = ACCEPT FAILURE. Schedule Year 2, 3, 5, 7 before you start.

🔍 Apply the framework: Dense gorse, 2ha, site slopes to creek, mid-spring (active growth). What method?
Diagnosis: Actively growing → systemic works. Near water → no chemical within 20m buffer. Solution: Cut stump on creek buffer zone (no chem). Basal bark on upper slope. Follow-up at 12 months.

Herbicide resistance · Real and growing

Resistance is not a future problem. It is happening now. Glyphosate-resistant annual ryegrass exists in Tasmania. The same WILL happen with woody weeds if we don't change practices.

💊 Predict: What happens when a contractor uses half-rate glyphosate over large areas for 3 consecutive years?
Outcome: Sub-lethal dosing selects resistant individuals. Surviving plants breed. Within 5 years, the population is no longer controlled by standard rates.
Correct: Always use label rates. Rotate mode of action (MOA) groups. Never spray the same herbicide on the same population year after year.
Early resistance signals: Patches within infestation that survive treatment while surrounding plants die. Same equipment, same mix, same day. That is not "missed spots" – that is resistance developing.

What NOT to do · The failure checklist

Memorise this. Print it. Put it in your vehicle.

  • ✗ Slash serrated tussock after seed set
  • ✗ Spray systemic herbicides during drought or frost
  • ✗ Assume one treatment = success
  • ✗ Ignore clean-down between sites
  • ✗ Treat without positive weed identification
  • ✗ Cut and leave willow branches in waterways
  • ✗ Use higher herbicide rate than label "for safety" (increases resistance selection)
  • ✗ Work alone in remote areas without PLB/EPIRB
  • ✗ Spray when wind >15km/h or during temperature inversion
  • ✗ Burn gorse without 12-month follow-up herbicide
The worst failure: Walking away after one treatment, believing the job is done. Seedbank + regrowth = reinfestation within 2-3 years.

Confidence traps · Where experienced operators fail

These are not beginner mistakes. These are the errors that catch skilled people because they look like success.

Trap 1: "Looks dead" ≠ dead

Gorse can look brown and dead above ground while root crowns are viable. Wait 6 months before declaring success. Resprouts are common.

Trap 2: "More chemical = better"

Exceeding label rates does not improve kill. It wastes chemical, increases environmental risk, and accelerates resistance selection. Label rate is scientifically determined optimum.

Trap 3: "Clean site = success"

No visible weeds does not mean no seedbank. The real infestation is underground. Control programs fail when they declare victory at Year 1 instead of Year 7.

Trap 4: "Past success predicts future"

What worked last season may fail this season. Weather, plant phenology, and resistance change. Always assess current conditions before applying.

Indigenous land management · Observation-based learning

Cultural burning vs uniform disturbance

Western fire management often treats entire blocks uniformly. Indigenous mosaic burning creates patchworks of different ages and burn intensities. This naturally suppresses woody weeds while maintaining habitat diversity.
Lesson: Consider patch-burn strategies, not whole-site treatments.

Reading the land, not the calendar

Instead of fixed dates ("spray in October"), Indigenous approaches read seasonal indicators: specific flowering events, insect activity, soil moisture, wind patterns. This is more reliable in variable Tasmanian climates.
Lesson: Use phenological signals (e.g., wattle flowering) as your treatment trigger.

Long-term thinking (decades, not seasons)

Weed control is often funded in 1-3 year cycles. Indigenous land management works in generational timeframes. The most successful weed programs are those that secure multi-year commitment.
Lesson: Advocate for 7-10 year follow-up plans from the beginning.

Micro-scenarios · 1-2 line decisions

🌿 You see gorse resprouting 6 months after treatment. What likely went wrong?

Low herbicide uptake (drought-stressed plant) OR missed stems during application. Resprouting is normal – need follow-up treatment.

💧 Customer wants you to spray willows along a trout stream. Chemical buffer is 20m. What do you do?

Cut stump mechanical removal within 20m. No chemical near water. Explain to customer why – legality and ecology.

📉 Your spray results are patchy. Some areas 100% kill, others 30%. Same mix, same day. What's likely happening?

Equipment issue (nozzle block, pressure variation) OR early resistance. Check calibration first. If consistent pattern, consider MOA rotation.

🔥 Fire has just swept through a gorse-infested valley. Landholder says "good, that's sorted." What do you say?

Fire stimulates gorse germination and resprouting. Will be worse within 12 months without follow-up herbicide. Plan now.

🚜 You find a threatened orchid patch underneath a gorse thicket you're treating. What's your immediate action?

STOP. Flag location. Do NOT spray. Hand-remove gorse around orchids. Report to land manager and NRE Tas. Legal protection overrides production.
